# Humor Guide

Human-Readable is not primarily about jokes. It is about recognition.

Readers should feel like they have overheard something true from a technical workplace, community lab, university department, garden shed, maker space, or ops channel and suddenly seen the invisible culture around it drawn on the page.

## Core Rule

Every comic begins with a real or realistically overheard sentence.

The sentence is source material, not necessarily the first line of dialogue.

The comic should answer:

> Why would someone say that?

Not:

> What does that sentence mean?

## The Human-Readable Pattern

A strong Human-Readable comic usually has:

1. A real sentence or phrase as the seed.
2. A normal scene where that sentence could plausibly arise.
3. One literal or visual exaggeration.
4. A quiet final beat.

The punchline should usually feel discovered, not delivered.

## What the Comic Is About

Human-Readable is about technical culture and the human systems around machines:

- institutional knowledge
- unwritten rules
- folklore
- pattern recognition
- literal thinking
- naming problems
- invisible social conventions
- systems that are logical but not humane
- people trying to make machine output meaningful

The machine is rarely the villain. Usually everyone involved is acting rationally from their own context.

## Readability

The comic should make implicit culture visible.

It can help technical people recognize shared experiences navigating workplaces and social systems where many expectations are left unsaid.

Do not turn that into a lecture. Instead, draw the assumptions, rituals, patterns, and context that were previously invisible.

## Tone

Prefer:

- dry
- understated
- affectionate
- technically literate
- observational
- lightly whimsical
- precise

Avoid:

- dunking on users
- generic programmer jokes
- explaining the punchline
- making every character sarcastic
- turning technical culture into a caricature
- making AI a default mascot

## Final Panel Rule

The last panel should often be quieter than the middle panel.

Good endings include:

- a small realization
- a tiny label
- a calm acceptance of chaos
- someone noticing the pattern
- a system doing exactly what was requested
- a human deciding the absurd thing is useful after all

## Example Seeds

> DHCP change on a Friday afternoon

This is not just about DHCP. It is about shared operational dread.

> Any event that specifies "bring your own jar" is pretty much guaranteed to be a good time

This is not just about jars. It is about recognizing a culture of practical, earnest, slightly niche people.

> Autogenerated UUID would've solved so many problems...

This is not just about identifiers. It is about the gap between machine-readable correctness and human-readable confidence.
