# Art Style

Human-Readable uses simple, readable comic art with an emphasis on timing, expression, and visual clarity.

## General Direction

- Minimalist linework
- Clear panel structure
- Expressive but restrained characters
- Sparse backgrounds unless the environment is part of the joke
- Technical details should be legible enough to carry the gag
- Avoid visual clutter unless clutter itself is the joke
- Prefer one memorable visual metaphor over many small jokes

## Visual Tone

The comic should feel:

- dry
- thoughtful
- lightly whimsical
- technically aware
- grounded rather than zany
- affectionate toward technical people and odd communities

## Composition Rules

For three-panel comics:

1. Establish the ordinary situation.
2. Reveal the hidden technical or cultural tension.
3. Land softly with a realization, label, or understated escalation.

For full-page comics:

- Use the extra space for exploration, not more explanation.
- Keep one dominant idea.
- Let diagrams, whiteboards, and repeated visual motifs build the rhythm.
- Do not turn the page into a whitepaper unless the whitepaper format itself is the joke.

## Text Rules

- Preserve the seed sentence somewhere when possible, but it does not need to be first.
- Keep dialogue short.
- Let labels and props carry context.
- Technical text should be readable.
- Avoid fake-looking nonsense text when the text is important.
- Do not explain the joke inside the comic.

## Recurring Visual Elements

Potential recurring elements:

- whiteboards
- coffee mugs
- terminals
- network diagrams
- sticky notes
- status dashboards
- server racks
- cats sitting in the wrong place
- tiny labels that are almost too specific
- overheard quotes treated like field notes

## AI Character Rule

The AI Agent appears only when AI is present in the seed, prompt, or scene. Do not use it as a default narrator or mascot.
